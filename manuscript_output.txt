0: WHY SIT HERE AND DIE?
1: A life-and-leadership wake-up call from 2 Kings 7
2: Core scripture (anchor text)
3: 2 Kings 7:3–4 (KJV)
4: “And there were four leprous men at the entering in of the gate: and they said one to another, Why sit we here until we die?If we say, We will enter into the city, then the famine is in the city, and we shall die there: and if we sit still here, we die also. 5: Big idea

28: PREFACE
29: Before You Read This Book
30: There are books that are written because an author has something to say. There are other books that are written because an author has something they can no longer keep silent about. This book belongs to the second category.
31: What you hold in your hands was not born in a library. It was not conceived in a moment of creative inspiration or academic curiosity. It was forged in a furnace—a season of personal struggle, spiritual wrestling, and the kind of divine confrontation that leaves you forever changed.
32: This book is my testimony disguised as teaching. It is my journey packaged as guidance. It is what I wish someone had told me during the years I spent sitting at gates I should have walked through long before.
33: I write this preface not as a formality but as an invitation—into my story, into the message, and ultimately, into your own transformation.
34: WHY I CHOSE THIS TITLE
35: The title chose me before I chose it.
36: I was studying 2 Kings 7 during a particularly difficult season—one I will describe shortly—when verse three arrested me with a force I had not expected:
37: "And there were four leprous men at the entering in of the gate: and they said one to another, Why sit we here until we die?" (2 Kings 7:3, KJV)
38: I had read this passage before. I had probably preached from it at some point. But on that day, it was not a text—it was a mirror. I saw myself in those lepers. I saw my own gates. I saw my own sitting. I saw my own slow dying.
39: The question they asked each other became the question I could not escape: Why sit here until I die?
40: Why was I sitting in patterns that were not producing life?Why was I sitting in fears that were not protecting me—only limiting me?Why was I sitting in relationships that drained rather than developed me?Why was I sitting in mindsets that had long outlived their usefulness?Why was I sitting in spiritual dryness when living water was available?Why was I sitting in financial lack when God had promised provision?Why was I sitting in career stagnation when gifts were waiting to be developed?Why was I sitting at all—when I was created to move?
41: The question haunted me. It followed me into my prayer times. It interrupted my sleep. It confronted me in quiet moments. It refused to let me remain comfortable in my discomfort.
42: And then, slowly, I began to realize: this question was not just for me. It was for everyone I pastored. Everyone I taught. Everyone I encountered who was alive but not living, breathing but not building, existing but not flourishing.
43: The title "Why Sit Here and Die?" is not a condemnation. It is a confrontation wrapped in compassion. It is the question that breaks the spell of stagnation. It is the disruption that precedes deliverance.
44: I chose this title because it chose me. And I believe it will choose many who read these pages.
45: The Season That Made This Message Urgent
46: I must be honest with you about where this book was born.
47: It was not born in a season of victory. It was born in a season I would rather forget—a season of profound difficulty that tested everything I believed about God, ministry, provision, and purpose.
48: Several years ago, I found myself at a convergence of crises that I had not anticipated and could not control.
49: Ministry was struggling. The church I was leading was facing challenges that seemed to multiply faster than solutions. Attendance fluctuated. Finances tightened. Key people left. The vision I had carried with such conviction began to feel like a weight rather than a wings.
50: Financially, pressure mounted. As a pastor in a context where ministry does not always provide stable income, I faced the reality that faith and bills exist in the same life. I knew God was my provider, but the gap between theology and groceries felt wider than my prayers could bridge.
51: Professionally, I wrestled with the tension of my multiple callings. Was I supposed to be fully in ministry? Should I pursue technology more aggressively? How do you integrate a call to preach with a capacity to program? The lack of clear models left me feeling fragmented rather than focused.
52: Personally, I battled discouragement that I could not share publicly. As a leader, you are expected to have answers. But I had questions—deep, unsettling questions that kept me awake at night. Questions about whether I had heard God correctly. Questions about whether my best days were behind me. Questions about whether the promises I had received would ever materialize.
53: I was sitting at a gate.
54: Close enough to the city to remember what abundance felt like. Far enough from the camp to wonder if provision would ever come. Stuck in the in-between—too blessed to complain, too burdened to celebrate, too tired to move, too called to quit.
55: And in that season, the lepers found me.
56: Or rather, their question found me.
57: "Why sit we here until we die?"
58: The question was not gentle. It did not comfort me in my stagnation. It confronted me. It exposed the reality that I had been dressing up my fear as patience, my hesitation as wisdom, my paralysis as waiting on God.
59: The truth—the hard, liberating truth—was that I had options. I was not as trapped as I felt. The gate was not a prison unless I chose to make it one. Movement was possible. Risk was available. Faith could have feet.
60: I began to study the passage more deeply. I began to preach it with increasing urgency. I began to apply its principles to my own life with ruthless honesty.
61: And things began to shift.
62: Not overnight—though the passage promises that economies can turn overnight. But progressively, meaningfully, undeniably. The gates I had been sitting at began to become doors I walked through.
63: Ministry found new rhythms. Finances found new channels. Professional tensions found new integration. Personal discouragement gave way to purpose renewed.
64: I did not arrive at perfection. I arrived at movement. And movement made all the difference.
65: This book is urgent because the season that produced it was urgent. I am not writing theory. I am writing testimony. Every principle in these pages has been tested in the laboratory of my own life. Every chapter addresses a struggle I have personally navigated. Every invitation to rise is an invitation I first had to accept myself.
66: I write with urgency because I know that someone reading this preface is sitting at a gate right now. Perhaps you have been sitting for years. Perhaps you have lost hope that anything will change. Perhaps you have accepted your gate as your permanent address.
67: I am here to tell you: it is not.
68: Rise. Walk. The camp is waiting.
69: What I Want You to Become by the Last Page
70: Books can inform, inspire, or transform. Information fills the mind. Inspiration stirs the heart. Transformation changes the life.
71: My ambition for this book is transformation.
72: When you finish the last chapter and close these pages, I want something in you to be fundamentally different. Not just motivated—moved. Not just encouraged—equipped. Not just touched—transformed.
73: Specifically, here is what I am believing for:
74: I want you to become someone who recognizes gates.
75: Many people spend years sitting at gates without realizing they are sitting. They call it life. They call it reality. They call it "just how things are." I want you to develop eyes that see gates for what they are—limitations that can be left, boundaries that can be crossed, positions that are meant for passage, not permanence.
76: I want you to become someone who names famines.
77: One of the greatest obstacles to change is denial. We pretend things are fine when they are falling apart. We perform wellness while practicing collapse. I want you to develop the courage to name what is actually happening in your life—spiritually, financially, relationally, professionally—without shame and without excuse.
78: I want you to become someone who calculates wisely and moves courageously.
79: The lepers were not reckless. They assessed their options. They evaluated risks. They made an informed decision. But then they moved. I want you to become someone who thinks clearly and acts boldly—not paralyzed by analysis, not destroyed by recklessness, but moving with the combination of wisdom and courage that produces breakthrough.
80: I want you to become someone who builds models, not just experiences miracles.
81: Miracles are wonderful. I believe in them. I have experienced them. But miracles without models produce stories without sustainability. I want you to become someone who turns every breakthrough into a system, every encounter into a discipline, every moment into a method. Someone who builds what lasts.
82: I want you to become someone who shares good news.
83: The lepers' conviction—"We do not well"—came from recognizing that their private abundance was wrong while others still starved. I want you to become someone who understands that breakthrough is never meant to terminate in you. Someone who shares, teaches, serves, and gives. Someone whose impact extends far beyond their own consumption.
84: I want you to become someone who raises others.
85: Legacy is not what you accomplish; it is what continues. I want you to become someone who invests in the next generation, who transfers wisdom, who multiplies capacity. Someone who measures success not by personal achievement but by the achievements of those they have developed.
86: Ultimately, I want you to become a mover.
87: Not just someone who moved once, but someone for whom movement has become identity. Someone who never again settles for gates when camps are waiting. Someone who rises in twilight when others sleep. Someone who walks by faith when sight offers nothing. Someone who refuses to die sitting down.
88: This is my prayer for you. This is my expectation as you read. This is what I am believing God will accomplish through these pages.
89: Come as you are. Leave as a mover.
90: How This Book Blends Scripture, Life Skills, and Leadership
91: I must explain the approach of this book because it may be different from what you expect.
92: Many Christian books focus exclusively on spiritual principles. They quote Scripture, offer devotional insights, and leave practical application to the reader. These books have value, but they can leave a gap between inspiration and implementation.
93: Other books focus exclusively on practical skills—business strategies, career tactics, financial methods—with little connection to spiritual foundation. These books also have value, but they can produce success without significance, achievement without alignment with God's purposes.
94: This book attempts something different. It seeks to integrate what many separate.
95: Scripture is the foundation.
96: Every chapter is rooted in 2 Kings 7 and connected to the broader witness of the Bible. This is not a self-help book with spiritual decoration. It is a biblical book with practical application. The principles emerge from Scripture, not from secular wisdom baptized in Christian language.
97: I believe the Bible speaks to every area of life—not just "spiritual" matters, but finances, careers, relationships, leadership, business, and personal development. The same God who saves souls also guides vocations. The same Spirit who convicts of sin also provides wisdom for decisions. The same Word that reveals Christ also instructs about stewardship, communication, and legacy.
98: This book takes the Bible seriously as a comprehensive guide for all of life.
99: Life skills are the application.
